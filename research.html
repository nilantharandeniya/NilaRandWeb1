Doctoral Research

The Institutional Mediation Principle

A framework explaining why organisations with the greatest responsibility for climate and disaster management show the lowest adoption of AI prediction technologies, despite comparable infrastructure to institutions that adopt faster.

Government agencies score 2.1 out of 5 on AI readiness. Academic institutions score 4.0 — on the same measurement framework, with comparable access to infrastructure and data. The Institutional Mediation Principle sets out why: adoption isn't primarily a technology problem, it's mediated by institutional structure, accountability regimes, and risk tolerance.

The research draws on 45 semi-structured interviews, five focus groups, and over 100 policy documents across three countries: the United Kingdom, Australia, and Sri Lanka. Fieldwork in Australia was carried out during a Visiting Research Fellowship at Western Sydney University's Centre for Smart Modern Construction.

One nuance that emerged directly from the data: higher organisational capacity doesn't always accelerate adoption. Mature, well-resourced institutions sometimes surface legitimate governance concerns that lower-capacity institutions miss entirely — meaning the mediation effect isn't uniformly positive. This boundary condition is part of the framework, not an exception to it.

The framework was presented and validated by 53 international experts at a Royal Academy of Engineering Frontiers Symposium in Tokyo, scoring 3.84 out of 4.0. A companion contribution, a dual-layer governance framework, sets out the design rationale for how institutions can build accountable AI systems rather than simply reviewing them after the fact.

Active Projects

Beyond the thesis

01

AI4CASCADE

Artificial Intelligence for Compound Climate and Health Cascade Predictions. A NERC Independent Research Fellowship application hosted at Loughborough University, modelling compound drought–flood–health cascades across the UK and Sri Lanka, with distinct biological endpoints per country. Built on the foundation of a Royal Academy of Engineering Frontiers Seed Funding project on co-designing ethical AI for seasonal rainfall forecasting.

02

Temperature Extremes & Respiratory Mortality

An explainable machine learning project detecting nonlinear and lagged impacts of UK temperature extremes on respiratory mortality, using a 676-row weekly dataset spanning 2010–2022. Joint work with Nelum Bopitiyegedara and Lahiru Kodithuwakku, presented at the GCPHR Annual Conference with full results due at UKADR 2026.

03

Urban Vulnerability in Sri Lanka

Two papers using AI-based urban morphological assessment: a polycentric vulnerability study of Colombo targeting Cities (Elsevier), and a comparative study of Kurunegala and Galle targeting the International Journal of Disaster Resilience in the Built Environment.

04

Flood-Driven Dengue Dynamics

An empirical study of lag relationships between flood events and dengue transmission in Sri Lanka, developed in collaboration with the National Building Research Organisation (NBRO), the Disaster Management Centre, and the Ministry of Health Epidemiology Unit.
